As a battery engineer specializing in warehouse automation, I have outlined the technical specifications for high-performance Autonomous Mobile Robot (AMR) power systems. In the warehouse environment, the shift from lead-acid to Lithium-ion (specifically LFP) has been driven by the need for high-intensity **opportunity charging**, which allows robots to operate 24/7 without battery swapping.

### Why Opportunity Charging (2C-3C) is the Defining Spec
In modern high-velocity warehouses, **uptime is the only metric that matters.** 
*   **Elimination of Swapping:** Manual battery swapping requires dedicated staff, charging rooms, and 2 batteries per robot.
*   **24/7 Availability:** By charging at **2C-3C**, an AMR can recover roughly 25-30% of its state-of-charge (SOC) in just 10 minutes. 
*   **Infrastructure Efficiency:** Fewer chargers are needed if robots can "sip" power rapidly during short idle windows rather than "gulping" power for 4-8 hours at a stationary dock.

---

### Key Trade-offs
1.  **Fast Charging vs. Cycle Life:** Charging at 3C generates significant heat. Without active thermal management or high-grade LFP cells, frequent fast-charging can degrade the SOH (State of Health) faster than the rated 3,000 cycles.
2.  **Energy Density vs. Safety:** NMC offers higher energy density (longer runtime in same space), but its lower thermal runaway temperature makes it a liability in high-density automated warehouses compared to LFP.
3.  **Weight vs. Traction:** Unlike EVs, AMRs sometimes *need* weight for traction when carrying heavy shelves. Replacing heavy lead-acid with light Lithium often requires adding "dead weight" (ballast) to the chassis.

### Failure Modes in AMR Applications
1.  **Dendrite Growth (Low-Temp Charging):** Attempting to fast-charge (2C+) at temperatures near 0°C without pre-heating causes lithium plating, leading to internal shorts.
2.  **Vibration-Induced Tab Failure:** Constant movement over warehouse expansion joints can cause ultrasonic welds on cell tabs to fatigue and snap.
3.  **BMS "Ghost" Drain:** If a robot is powered down for weeks, the BMS idle current can over-discharge the cells to 0V, bricking the pack.
4.  **Inaccurate SOC Drift:** Constant shallow opportunity charging (e.g., staying between 40% and 80%) prevents the BMS from seeing "full" or "empty" states, causing the SOC estimate to drift over time.
