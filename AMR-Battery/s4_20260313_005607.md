### Why Standard Industrial LFP Batteries Fail GMP Requirements

Standard industrial LiFePO4 (LFP) batteries, while chemically stable, are insufficient for pharmaceutical and medical deployments for three primary reasons:
1.  **Particulate Generation:** Industrial packs often use active fan cooling or vented enclosures (IP20-IP54). In an ISO 5 cleanroom, the air turbulence and micro-particles from internal PCB outgassing are prohibited.
2.  **Data Integrity:** Standard BMS protocols (CANopen/SMBus) lack the cryptographic signatures and immutable logging required by **FDA 21 CFR Part 11**. Without a "chained" history of the battery’s health linked to a specific production batch, the AMR cannot be used in validated pharmaceutical manufacturing.
3.  **Material Outgassing:** The plastics used in standard batteries often "gas off" volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that can contaminate sensitive drug formulations or biological samples.

---

### Key Trade-offs
*   **Thermal Management vs. Sealing:** A hermetically sealed IP67 pack cannot use fans. This requires heavy aluminum heat sinks or phase-change materials (PCM), increasing weight and reducing the AMR's payload/range.
*   **Fast Charging vs. Longevity:** Charging at >1C in a cleanroom generates localized heat, which may trigger HVAC alarms or degrade the cleanroom's temperature stability. GMP environments typically prefer "Slower-In-Situ" or "Battery Swap" in a dedicated gray-space.

### Failure Modes in Medical/GMP Context
1.  **VHP Ingress:** Hydrogen Peroxide vapor is highly penetrative. If the seal fails, VHP can corrode BMS traces, leading to a "ghost" shutdown or false SOC readings during critical transport.
2.  **BMS Lock-out:** To comply with safety standards, a medical BMS may "brick" the battery if a single cell deviates. In a hospital, this creates an obstruction (dead robot) in high-traffic corridors.
3.  **Surface Degradation:** Standard ABS plastics will stress-crack after 30 days of IPA exposure, leading to bacterial traps (bioburden) that fail environmental monitoring (EM) swabs.
